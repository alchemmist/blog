There is a curious fact about circles on water: in these little waves the water, relative to its own plane, moves literally nowhere. It merely oscillates up and down according to a certain rhythm. And this illustrates well both our consciousness and the world as a whole.
![|400](/images/circles-on-water.png)
For the world is created out of the ocean of non-being. One might think it is somehow hewn out of it, or that this non-being is somehow condensed into something, and that God needs a thousand instruments for this. But no. There is only an imperceptibly gentle touch and the vibrations of nothingness under his energies, endlessly spreading outward to that well-known sound *om*. This is the whole world — the ever-granted, second-by-second appearance of this simple and light touch, spreading out from the center, from the Good.

The state of the spirit is often likened to clear water. Why so? It might seem somehow bland or dull. But it is precisely against the background of plain water that all tastes unfold. That is, clear water possesses an infinite sensitivity in this respect. Against its background any taste or aroma, even the subtlest, becomes a melody. And the space between the notes is filled by that very same water, which is, in essence, an absence.

And from this picture it is interesting to see that this absence is infinitely sensitive — not only in the plane of taste and aroma, but literally. That is, it is impossible to interact with water without leaving circles upon it. However light your touch may be. Or, perhaps, your step. If there is enough faith in you, the water will surely answer it with circles.

Incidentally, this is very nearly the only genuine circle — genuine precisely in its dynamics. Because if you take some other circle, say the one I can trace with a twig in the sand, then at some discrete point it may seem to be a circle. But in the dance of time this is not so. For I will draw it as a line that breaks off, whereas a true circle does not break. And circles on water are exactly like this: they are born and forever spread outward precisely as circles, because they have no rupture at any point. Here one may recall Cusanus, for it is precisely circles on water, if set loose in a sufficiently vast ocean of eternity, that would unite within themselves the infinite circle, and the infinite line, and the point — and, in essence, whatever infinite thing you please.

What also delights me in this picture is that moss shows through the water at the bottom. It is interesting how the same scene would look from its point of view. For circles on water, it seems, are visible even when you are underwater. And moreover, both of these spaces, the above-water and the below-water, lie on opposite sides of the watery mirror, and it is not clear what separates them. That is, it is some imperceptibly thin boundary of the water's surface. One might say it is surface tension, but it is not some oily film. It is literally made of water. And it is precisely this that responds to those vibrations, reshaping the landscape of both the aqueous and the aerial space.

And it seems to me that this, in a certain sense, is the human being. That is, you know, at first glance it might seem that the human is somewhere off-frame: here is his hand, and further off, somewhere, is his body. But the human body is only the image of the agency of his soul. Truly the human is present as just such an imperceptible watery surface. A soap bubble. Or a crystal, if you will — one that somehow lets the light pass through itself. And the only thing we as humans can influence is how this light passes through us. How cleanly, how softly, how smoothly, how clearly, how gently, how finely, how long, how deeply. This inner landscape, which is not the space beneath it (that is, the inner world) nor the space above it (that is, external reality), but precisely this very boundary. In this sense the holographic principle represents our capacity — the eternal soul's — to operate within time.

And this is quite interesting. It turns out that both the inner world and the outer one can take any form, for they are in essence empty, and emptiness, as we know, contains within itself a thousand forms. And this form is taken in the rays of light that pass through this inner prism.

The body and the hand here are needed more for illustration. Like, you know, in interface design, where they draw all sorts of animations and the like. For when you tap an icon, one could simultaneously repaint all the pixels on the screen and display the window — there are no technical constraints against this. But we are not used to it. And so they draw animations, where these pixels are smoothly painted in in a certain sequence. So too in being, so too in this picture. We are not used to seeing water simply stir on its own — simply because it has a free will. And so we are given hands and feet, with which the animation of causality is drawn. Because it is more familiar to us that way.

And so it is still not clear, as they say, science has not yet discovered: whether it is the finger, touching the water, that sends circles across it, or the watery eye that opens to look at who has touched it.
